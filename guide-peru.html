Peru is on the calendar for late 2025 — Machu Picchu, Cusco, the Sacred Valley, and possibly Lima. This guide will go live after the trip with real notes, photos, and recommendations.

Below is a preview of the regions I'm planning to cover and what I'm most excited about.

Regions

Peru is enormous and wildly varied — from coastal desert to Amazon jungle to Andean highlands. The trip is focused on the highlands, which is where most of the historic sites and the most dramatic landscapes are.

Cusco

The Inca capital — 3,400m above sea level, impossible to rush.

Cusco was the center of the Inca Empire and is still one of the most historically rich cities in the Americas. The altitude hits hard — most people need 1–2 days just to acclimatize before doing anything else. The city itself is worth exploring slowly: colonial architecture built directly on Inca stone foundations, incredible food, and a base for everything in the region.

🏛️ Qorikancha (Inca Temple of the Sun) 🏰 Sacsayhuamán fortress 🍽️ San Pedro Market 🌆 Plaza de Armas

The Sacred Valley

Inca ruins, Andean villages, and extraordinary mountain scenery.

The Sacred Valley runs between Cusco and Machu Picchu and is worth at least a full day on its own — it's not just a transit corridor. Ollantaytambo and Pisac have impressive ruins, local markets, and a pace of life that's completely different from Cusco. At lower altitude than Cusco, which makes it easier to acclimatize.

🏰 Ollantaytambo ruins 🛍️ Pisac market 🌄 Andean mountain views 🚞 Train to Aguas Calientes

Machu Picchu

One of the most extraordinary places on earth. Worth every logistic.

Machu Picchu requires planning — timed entry tickets sell out months in advance, and you need to book the train to Aguas Calientes separately. The complexity is worth it. The scale and setting of the ruins, perched in the cloud forest between two mountain peaks, is genuinely unlike anything else in the world.

🏔️ Machu Picchu citadel ⛰️ Huayna Picchu hike (book early) 🌫️ Sun Gate (Inti Punku) 🚌 Bus from Aguas Calientes

Lima

One of the world's great food cities — often an afterthought, shouldn't be.

Most people treat Lima as a layover before heading to Cusco. That's a mistake. Lima has arguably the best restaurant scene in South America — Peruvian cuisine is extraordinary and Lima is where the best of it concentrates. Miraflores and Barranco are the neighborhoods worth spending time in.

🍽️ World-class restaurant scene 🌊 Miraflores clifftop park 🎨 Barranco arts district 🏛️ Larco Museum

Planning notes

Things I'm researching ahead of the trip — will be updated with real experience after.

  • ↳ Machu Picchu timed entry tickets need to be booked months in advance — this is not optional. Same for the Huayna Picchu hike.
  • ↳ Altitude sickness (soroche) is real at Cusco's elevation of 3,400m. Plan 1–2 acclimatization days before doing anything strenuous.
  • ↳ The dry season (May–September) is the best time to go — Machu Picchu in cloud and mist is beautiful but you want at least one clear day.
  • ↳ Don't skip Lima just to catch a connection. Even 2 nights there is enough to understand why Peruvian food is in a class of its own.
