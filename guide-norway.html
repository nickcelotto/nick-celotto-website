Below is a preview of the regions I'm planning to cover and what I'm most excited to see.

Regions

Norway is long and thin — about the same north-to-south distance as the US east coast. Each region is dramatically different. The plan is focused on the west coast fjords and the far north.

Oslo

The capital — compact, design-forward, and expensive.

Oslo is the obvious entry point and worth 2–3 days on its own. The city is walkable, has excellent museums (the Munch and the Viking Ship Museum are both genuinely impressive), and sits at the end of the Oslofjord with good views in every direction. It's one of the more expensive cities in Europe — budget accordingly.

🎨 Munch Museum ⛵ Viking Ship Museum 🏛️ The Opera House (walk on the roof) 🌊 Oslofjord 🌲 Holmenkollen ski jump & forest

Bergen & the Fjords

The gateway to the most dramatic landscape in Europe.

Bergen is a small, colorful city on the west coast that serves as the base for fjord exploration. The famous Nærøyfjord and Sognefjord are accessible from here — both are UNESCO World Heritage sites and the scale of them in person is hard to describe. Take the Flåm Railway while you're in the region; it's one of the most scenic train rides in the world.

🏘️ Bryggen wharf (UNESCO) 🚞 Flåm Railway 🏔️ Nærøyfjord ⛴️ Fjord cruises 🌄 Trolltunga hike

Lofoten Islands

The most photogenic place in Norway — possibly in the world.

The Lofoten Islands are above the Arctic Circle and have a landscape that doesn't look real — dramatic peaks rising directly from the sea, traditional red fishing villages (rorbuer), and in winter, the best northern lights viewing in Europe. Requires a flight from Oslo or Bergen, but it's absolutely worth it.

🌌 Northern lights (Nov–Feb) 🏔️ Reinebringen hike 🎣 Reine fishing village 📸 Nusfjord (most photographed village) 🌅 Midnight sun (Jun–Jul)

Tromsø

The best base for northern lights in the country.

Tromsø is the largest city north of the Arctic Circle and the main base for northern lights tours. It has a surprisingly good food scene and cultural life for its size. In winter you get polar night (sun barely rises) and in summer you get midnight sun — both are worth experiencing.

🌌 Northern lights tours 🐋 Whale watching (winter) ⛪ Arctic Cathedral 🎿 Dog sledding

Planning notes

Things I'm researching ahead of the trip — will be updated with real experience after.

  • ↳ Norway is extremely expensive — easily the most expensive country I'll have visited. Budget roughly 2x what you'd expect elsewhere in Europe for accommodation and food.
  • ↳ The northern lights are not guaranteed — you need clear skies and solar activity. Going for at least 5–7 nights significantly improves your odds.
  • ↳ The fjords are best in summer for hiking and visibility, but winter has the aurora and a completely different (and equally spectacular) character.
  • ↳ The Norway in a Nutshell train route (Bergen–Flåm–Myrdal–Oslo) is one of the most efficient ways to see the fjords and doesn't require a car.
